Flattening the Curve¶

Problem Statement¶

As demonstrated by these past few months, epidemic diseases can have drastic effects on people all around the world. As governments haved rushed to respond to COVID-19, we have also seen that not only are lives affected through the sickness itself, but the economic impacts have also strained society. In order to help limit the spread of the disease while keeping the economy as funictioning as possible, non-pharmaceutical interventions (NPIs) can be an effective way to keep people safe while scientists find a cure.

In response, we have decided to look at a strategy to determine the least number of groups to close that minimizes the chance of spreading the infection while allowing society to function as much as possible. We also know that our hospital can only hold up to 50 people and that infected people will need a bed there from day 7 to day 10 of their infection. As such, our ultimate goal is to find the maximum group headcount to open while keeping peak hospitalization within capacity.

Data¶

In order to perform our analysis, we looked at data under three scenarios of government intervention. The following datasets contain data corresponding to peak hospitalization at each stage of intervention:

  • Early Intervention has 2 initially infected people
  • Late Intervention has 20 initially infected people
  • Very Late Intervention has 40 initially infected people

Variables¶

These datasets contain valuable information about the following variables

  • x_values: headcount threshold of a group to close
  • max_bed_demand: largest number of beds required throughout the entire simulation of a virus epidmemic

Analysis¶

Given this data for the three scenarios, we analyzed the correlation between the minimum headcount in a closed group and the maximum bed demand under each scenario.

For the following graphs, note that the red line represents the peak hospital capacity at 50 beds.

As we can see from this scatterplot, as the minimum headcount in a closed group rises, so too does the maximum bed demand. Given our threshold of maximum bed demand as 50, we can look at the data to find the maximum headcount to allow for an open group.

In [5]:
max_x_2 = data_2[ data_2.max_bed_demand <= 50 ].x_values.max()
max_x_2
Out[5]:
120

From our data, we have determined for early intervention that the maximum headcount in an open group should be less than 120 people.

As the maximum group size in our test case is 119, this means that if we can intervene early enough such that the number of infected is still at 2, there will be no requirement of extended group closures.

Recommendation¶

Given our analysis, we recommend that government officials and other key decision makers implement or recommend the following guidelines when structuring their NPI policies:

  1. Explain the importance of NPIs to the general public.
  2. Assess the stage of intervention.
    • if there are around 2 people already infected, then it is early stage
    • if there are around 20 people already infected, then it is late stage
    • if there are around 40 people already infected, then it is very late stage
  3. Set limits on the maximum size of groups that are allowed to meet.
    • Less than 120 people for early stage (allows all groups to meet)
    • Less than 39 people for late stage
    • Less than 11 people for very late stage
  4. Publish these guidelines and enforce them.
  5. Continuously assess the situation. There will always be external factors, such as a surge in other ailments that may decrease the capcity within hospitals. Also, these recommendations are based on simulations which can not guarantee 100% accuracy. As such, it is important to adjust as situations change.
